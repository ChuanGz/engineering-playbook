# Decision Records

## Decision supported

Preserve the forces and consequences behind a consequential choice so it can be operated, reviewed, and superseded using evidence rather than memory.

## When to record

Create a decision record when a choice is expensive to reverse, affects multiple owners, changes a contract or quality attribute, accepts material risk, or repeatedly attracts the same debate.

Do not record routine implementation choices that are obvious from code and cheap to replace.

## Required content

1. Status, date, scope, and accountable owner.
2. Context and evidence available when the decision was made.
3. Prioritized drivers and non-goals.
4. Credible alternatives under the same constraints.
5. Decision and positive and negative consequences.
6. Validation plan, follow-up actions, and reconsideration triggers.

Label assumptions. Do not invent metrics, incidents, or project constraints to make the record look complete.

## Lifecycle

Keep accepted records immutable except for corrections and status links. When context changes, create a new record that supersedes the old one and explain what evidence changed.

## Trade-offs

Records reduce context loss and repeated debate but become noise when every choice is documented. Apply rigor in proportion to consequence and reversal cost.

## Failure modes

- Writing a decision announcement with no alternatives.
- Recording only advantages of the selected option.
- Editing history until the original rationale disappears.
- Keeping accepted actions without owners or validation.

## Review evidence

- [ ] Alternatives were credible under the same constraints.
- [ ] Negative consequences and accepted risks are explicit.
- [ ] Validation and reconsideration use observable evidence.
- [ ] Supersession history remains traceable.
